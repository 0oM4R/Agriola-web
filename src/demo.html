How to use the interactive map?

>

1

Land Degradation Problem

● User first detect the area of interest(part of the delta) then choose from slider type of analysis (in this case loss analysis), a bar chart will show the losses of vegetation area from 2000 to 2020.

● By using three mentioned bands above the user can see on delta layer that represent the vegetation area(in green color- indicate the areas that did not change- ), the current losses area (in red color),( the gained areas (in blue color).

2

NDVI Analysis

● User first detect the area of interest(part of the delta) then choose from slider type of analysis (in this case NDVI analysis), a chart will show the Normalized Difference Vegetation Index from 2000 to 2021.

By using NDVI band above, This index defines values from -1.0 to 1.0, Calculations of NDVI for a given pixel always result in a number that ranges from minus one (-1) to plus one (+1); however, no green leaves gives a value close to zero. A zero means no vegetation and close to +1 (0.8 - 0.9) indicates the highest possible density of green leaves.

3

Water Occurrence Change Intensity

● This provides a measure of how surface water has changed between two epochs: 1984-1999 and 2000-2015.

● The water occurrence layer has two colors: Red/blue indicates a decrease/increase in surface water occurrence between the epochs. .

● We use the defined region of interest to calculate a histogram of the changing intensity for the ROI sampling at a 30m scale.

4

Water Class Transition

● This layer captures changes between three classes of water occurrence (not water, seasonal water, and permanent water) along with two additional classes for ephemeral water (ephemeral permanent and ephemeral seasonal).

● For this analysis, a pie chart is made to better summarize the relatives’ size of the water transition classes.

5

Visualizing changing landscapes

● This animation used data JRC Yearly Water Classification History contains maps of the location and temporal distribution of surface water from 1984 to 2018 and provides statistics on the extent and change of those water surfaces. These data were generated using 3,865,618 scenes from Landsat 5, 7, and 8 acquired between 16 March 1984 and 31 December 2018. Each pixel was individually classified into water / non-water.

● We use river dynamics to demonstrate fading and cumulative history animation effects.

● There are two ways in animation to represent the water surface change:

First: Fading history where a single year’s channel is shown per frame and fade filters accumulate making the earliest pixels more volatile.
Second: Cumulative history animation shows the full river channel history by removing the fade filter image overlay and assigning a distinct color to each year’s river channel. They are colored according to year by stretching a palette across the range of years.
